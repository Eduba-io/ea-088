The frame

Delta's centennial year closed with a record $4.6 billion in free cash flow, a new leadership bench in Atlanta, and the loudest airline AI governance story in the US market still unfolding. The Fetcherr rollout works. The public narrative around it has drifted ahead of the substance. The operational resiliency work after July 2024 is the other half of the same conversation.

We work with corporate innovation teams inside large carriers. The work is called computational orchestration. It is the discipline of deciding which part of a problem belongs on an AI layer, which belongs on traditional code, which belongs on rule-based logic, and which belongs to human judgment. For most of the problems inside an airline, the split is roughly 60 percent traditional code, 30 percent rules, 10 percent genuine AI. Teams that skip the distinction put LLMs where Postgres would do the job faster, cheaper, and with better guarantees.

Three concrete reads

  1. AI pricing is not the governance problem

    Aggregated-data pricing is a well-understood rules-and-forecasting layer with a thin AI recommender on top. What is missing is the auditable artifact that a senator's staffer or an FTC economist could read in ten minutes and close the file. That artifact is a deliverable, not a product.

  2. Post-CrowdStrike resiliency is an orchestration problem

    Buying a new crew scheduler is a 2028 outcome. Designing the layer above the scheduler so that a future outage absorbs inside the technology instead of cascading into the operation is a 2026 outcome.

  3. TechOps documentation is where LLM work lands or gets shelved

    The reason it gets shelved is that the context around the model is not interpretable. Fix the context architecture, and the tools become ones the airworthiness engineer will sign off on.

From your eMerge Americas talk this afternoon, Tim

Matt sat in on your eMerge Americas session. Several themes you raised mapped cleanly to how we scope this work. A few mapped to where we have delivered.

Discretionary effort as the real outcome

You framed workforce technology as the space between the system and the person using it, and you were direct that discretionary effort is the thing you are trying to unlock. We treat that as the only adoption metric that counts. Thirty days after training, 95 percent of Pacific Life and Colgate-Palmolive trainees are still in the tools. The reason is that the training teaches a decision model, not a click path. Training you can keep is training that changes behavior.

“Ability to learn feels like there’s a monopoly on it.” Tim Gregory, eMerge Americas 2026, this afternoon

The line landed in the room. Our reading is that the monopoly is an artifact of how learning tools are built, not a fact about who can learn. The Ethics Engine paper is one piece of the answer. We publish the psychometric scaffolding so any enterprise can audit a model with the same rigor it would audit a person, and then hand that audit to a line worker in the same form it hands it to a VP.

Small models, internal reward signal

You were precise on this one. A 2 million parameter model calibrated to the enterprise's own reward signal will beat a trillion-parameter model that cannot see the enterprise. Our orchestration layer sits here. It is what lets a team tell how well the system is doing on its own terms, today, and tune it for tomorrow. This is the governance artifact that closes the file for a senator's staffer, a TechOps airworthiness review, or an internal HR audit.

Agentic scripts are painful

We agree, loudly. Most agent architectures collapse because the context handed to the agent is not interpretable, not auditable, and not shaped to the business. Eduba publishes a correction to this under the name Interpretable Context Methodology, or ICM. It treats context as a first-class asset that a workforce technology leader can read, revise, and own. Agents are downstream of that, and they work better when the context above them is legible.

Job hierarchy, compensation, and what training costs really are

The point you made about workforce AI colliding with comp structure is one we have hit elsewhere. Training is the real challenge, and the reason it is hard is that the enterprise has to decide who gets reskilled up a tier and who gets enabled inside their tier. The tooling has to respect both paths. The precedent in the next section is exactly this shape.
